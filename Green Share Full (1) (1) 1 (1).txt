ABSTRACT
This project addresses the critical environmental and social challenge of resource wastage and the increasing volume of usable goods ending up in landfills, which causes significant ecological and economic damage. There is an urgent need for a fast, intelligent, and trustworthy mechanism to facilitate the redistribution of these items within the community. This inspired the development of GreenShare, a dedicated mobile application designed to establish a seamless, circular economy platform.
The GreenShare mobile system operates by allowing donors to upload items, which are then immediately processed by an Artificial Intelligence (AI) model trained to recognize and categorize the goods. When an item is successfully categorized and listed, the application provides real-time, location-based access to recipients. The system is further secured by a robust rating and history log to ensure accountability and build community trust.
The project will be developed using Agile/Scrum  management framework to accommodate changes in the requirements and stakeholder’s feedback. . The  proposed system is expected to  significantly protect the environment by promoting reuse, save natural resources, and provide local communities with a reliable, transparent tool for rapid, ethical resource sharing via their smartphones.

1.1 Introduction :
Think of sustainability as being a good neighbor to the Earth and to each other. It means we use what we need today without taking away what our children will need tomorrow. For a long time, we have simply thrown things away when we were done with them. This linear approach has proven to be a waste. The circular economy presents a superior paradigm. It is a simple promise: we will stop making trash. Instead, we will prioritize the repair, reuse, and remanufacturing of goods, ensuring everything we use has a long, useful life. This shift is essential for maintaining a clean and healthy planet, representing the most valuable legacy we can secure for future generations.

1.2Problem Definition:
Jordan suffers from problems of excessive waste and weak reusability of usable items. Some people in Jordan throw away clothes, books, furniture, and other household items, even though they are still in good condition. At the same time, other individuals, families, and charities are in need of these goods. Although donation programs exist in Jordan, they are scattered, mostly manual, and lack wide reach. Currently, there is no simple online platform in Jordan that allows people who want to donate usable items to easily connect with those who need them. There is also no system that helps categorize items, match donors and recipients based on location, or organize pickup processes.
1. Wastage of reusable resources that could benefit others
2. Growing pressure on landfills and waste management systems(such as :Amman Municipality)
3. Missed opportunities to strengthen social solidarity and community support
4. Poor visibility of donation needs and opportunities across regions
5. Lack of data and measurable indicators for reuse and sustainable consumption
According to the Jordan Ministry of Environment’s Waste Sector Green Growth Plan (2020–2030), the recycling rate of municipal solid waste in Jordan remains low, at approximately 7%. In addition, Jordanians generate an average of 0.81 kg of municipal waste per capita per day, resulting in more than 2 million tons of municipal solid waste annually (Jordan Ministry of Environment, 2020). These figures highlight an urgent need to shift from waste disposal practices toward reuse and recycling, thereby promoting environmental responsibility and improving resource efficiency
The lack of a modern, userFriendly digital system limits the ability of individuals to donate easily and of organizations to manage, track, and analyze donations effectively. This digital gap is particularly significant in a time when mobile applications dominate daily life and technology can play a major role in enabling sustainability.

1.6 Literature Review:

1.6.1 Introduction:
Platforms for sustainable resource-sharing and circular economy refer to the use of digital technologies to facilitate the sharing and reuse of resources such as electronic devices and furniture among individuals, which strengthens the circular economy and reduces waste. This concept emerged as a response to environmental and social challenges caused by overconsumption and inadequate waste-management systems.
While developed countries have successfully implemented these platforms, developing countries and the Middle East face significant barriers, including limited infrastructure, insufficient digital literacy, security concerns, lack of trust mechanisms, and the absence of integrated solutions that address both technical and social dimensions. Many existing platforms also focus solely on perceived acceptance without measuring actual behavioral change or sustained user engagement.

This literature review examines recent research on digital platforms for sustainable resource-sharing, with a focus on mobile applications for device donation, sustainable education, and community furniture-sharing platforms. The review identifies research gaps and shows how innovative technological solutions such as AI-based quality verification, authentication systems, and direct communication mechanisms can address these gaps and enable more effective and sustainable resource-sharing systems.

1.6.2 Digital Platforms and Technological Integration
Research into digital platforms for the circular economy often focuses on the technical architecture and the integration of advanced technologies to enhance efficiency and transparency. For instance, the "Recycle Chain" project demonstrated the feasibility of using Blockchain, Image Processing (CNN, YOLO), and IoT to create a transparent and rewarding recycling system, although it highlighted the need for costly hardware for high-accuracy AI (Borandag, 2023). Similarly, the development of deep-learning frameworks has proven highly effective in enhancing the classification of waste products, with models like ResNet50V2 achieving high accuracy (Al-Saqqar et al., 2023).
From a system architecture perspective, the concept of a cloud-based Product-Service System (PSS) platform has been proposed to manage household solid waste, aiming to solve the "three-highs" problem (cost, risk, threshold) of smart equipment and enhance stakeholder enthusiasm (Wan et al., 2020). This PSS model, which focuses on providing a service facilitated by a product, is analogous to GreenShare's approach of providing a sharing/donation service via a mobile application. Furthermore, the efficiency of advanced digital platforms in social service administration, such as those benchmarked in European contexts, provides evidence that high automation (80-95%) and usage rates (85-95%) are achievable, correlating positively with sustainability indices (Sarojin et al., 2025).
In terms of platform functionality, the development of mobile applications to digitize the donation process, such as "DonoSync," has been shown to enhance data accuracy and improve donor/organization trust by providing end-to-end tracking (Sani & Rejab, 2024). However, the development of critical features like map interfaces and real-time notification functions can be technically challenging, as noted in the development of the "RescuePlate" application for food donation (Jacildo & Soberano, 2024).

1.6.3 User Acceptance and Behavioral Focus
User acceptance and behavioral intention are critical factors for the success of sharing economy platforms. Studies have consistently shown that user participation dilemmas, particularly those related to trust, social presence, and lax platform governance, inhibit engagement in peer-to-peer (P2P) product sharing (He et al., 2021). The challenge of building trust is further emphasized in the context of sharing furniture and home decoration products, where doubts about item condition and image quality are critical barriers (Sarunporn & Jitiporn, 2020).
The Extended Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT2) model has been widely used to analyze user behavior in this domain. Research on food waste reduction platforms confirmed the relevance of UTAUT2 factors but identified two new, context-specific factors: trust and gratefulness (Moltene & Orsato, 2021). Effort expectancy was also found to be a key factor for use behavior. Furthermore, a study on digital technology adoption for agricultural waste revealed that while standard motivators had no significant impact, older users were driven by trust and social influence, while younger users were motivated by data privacy (Pienwisetkaew et al., 2023).
Finally, the mobile application approach for donation has been validated by case studies, such as one focusing on electronic device donation, which achieved an 80% donation rate in an experimental group (Zhang & Sahagun, 2023). Additionally, the use of gamification in mobile applications has been shown to be an effective tool for promoting the Circular Economy and achieving moderate-to-high acceptance rates among users (José Antonio et al., 2025).




1.6.4 Research Gaps and GreenShare's Contribution

The synthesis of the literature reveals a significant research gap that the "GreenShare" project is uniquely positioned to address: the lack of an integrated, community-focused platform that simultaneously addresses the technological requirements of item classification and the behavioral requirements of user trust and participation in the sharing of reusable goods.
Firstly, a fragmentation of digital solutions exists, where platforms focus narrowly on specific areas like recycling (Borandag, 2023; Al-Saqqar et al., 2023), food waste (Moltene & Orsato, 2021), or general donation (Sani & Rejab, 2024). Our project "GreenShare"unifies the process of sharing and donation of diverse reusable goods within a single, integrated platform, promoting a broader circular economy model (Wan et al., 2020).
Secondly, there is a lack of integrated trust mechanisms in many technological solutions, despite behavioral studies emphasizing that trust and social presence are critical for P2P sharing adoption (He et al., 2021). GreenShare directly mitigates this by integrating a comprehensive Rating System and Direct Messaging to foster social presence and build community trust (Moltene & Orsato, 2021; Pienwisetkaew et al., 2023; Sarunporn & Jitiporn, 2020).
Finally, while AI is proven for waste classification (Al-Saqqar et al., 2023), there is a clear gap in applying high-accuracy AI for the classification and condition verification of reusable items for sharing/donation. GreenShare utilizes AI Image Recognition to automatically classify items and verify item condition, reducing the effort expectancy for donors and improving the search/filter efficiency for recipients (Borandag, 2023; Sarunporn & Jitiporn, 2020). Furthermore, GreenShare prioritizes the robust implementation of Location-Based Services and System Notification Generation, learning from the technical difficulties faced by similar projects (Jacildo & Soberano, 2024).
1.6.5 Conclusion
Based on the comprehensive review of recent literature, digital technologies such as mobile applications, AI, IoT, and Blockchain have proven essential in advancing the Circular Economy and transforming public service delivery, yet significant barriers remain. While individual studies demonstrated success in specific sectors—such as classifying waste with 98.95% accuracy using ResNet50V2 , increasing device donation rates to 80% via mobile apps , or managing food rescue operations —they consistently encountered critical user-centric and technical challenges, including a lack of trust in data privacy, the high cost of hardware for AI processing, and demographic disparities where "one-size-fits-all" solutions failed to engage both older and younger generations effectively. Furthermore, a major research gap persists in the fragmentation of these systems; governmental social services, non-profit donation platforms, and municipal environmental management systems currently operate in isolation, missing the opportunity for a unified exchange mechanism. Consequently, this project proposes an integral, unified framework that bridges these disjointed sectors by incorporating multi-layered authentication and AI-based verification to address the identified trust deficits and technical limitations, thereby ensuring a more efficient, transparent, and sustainable ecosystem for all stakeholders.

1.9 Proposed Scope:
This section outlines what the project is and activities to be conducted to achieve the goal of the project in general. The project will deliver a  mobile application for free donations and reuse of items in Jordan. The scope covers requirements gathering, design, implementation and testing required to develop the core functionalities: user registration, posting donation ads, browsing listings, and private in-app messaging for pickup coordination. Crucially, the project will integrate Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Image Recognition technology to automatically classify donated items upon photo upload, enhancing user experience and data quality. The project will be implemented using an Agile methodology, and it shall undergo comprehensive functional testing. 
1.10 Scope Excluded and Project Constraints:
This section explains the constraints that will be surrounding the project and the features that will not be covered in the current phase. The application will not include any token-based systems for user commitment (like the "Bananas" model) nor will it provide third-party logistics services for item pickup. Time constraints dictate that the project must be completed within academic semester, which may limit the depth of the AI/Image Recognition model training and require the team to focus on a basic classification set. Financial constraints may limit the ability to acquire premium cloud services for hosting the AI model, potentially affecting its speed and scalability under heavy load. Technical constraints mean that while AI is included, the initial model will be limited to broad category classification (e.g., "Furniture," "Clothing") and will not perform fine-grained analysis (e.g., "Brand," "Condition"). Additionally, resource constraints related to team experience may necessitate a longer development cycle for the AI integration, potentially delaying the final deployment

Brainstorming  
For the Green Share project, we approached requirements gathering as a process of discovery rather than just documentation. Since our goal is to shift community behavior towards sustainability, we needed to generate ideas that would genuinely motivate people to change their habits. We started by identifying the core problem: the gap between people who have unwanted usable items and the people in need who could benefit from them. We focused on two main objectives: first, how to educate users about the environmental value of second-hand goods, and second, how to drastically simplify the donation process so it becomes effortless for the donor. This brainstorming clarified our targeted requirements.
